administratively lihou forms part of the parish of st peter s in the bailiwick of guernsey and is now owned by the parliament of guernsey the states of guernsey although there have been a number of owners in the past since 2006 the island has been jointly managed by the guernsey environment department and the lihou charitable trust in the past the island was used by locals for the collection of seaweed for use as a fertiliser but today lihou is mainly used for tourism including school trips lihou is also an important centre for conservation forming part of a ramsar wetland site for the preservation of rare birds and plants as well as historic ruins of a priory and a farmhouse in common with several nearby islands such as jethou and brecqhou the name contains the norman suffix hou which means a small hill or a mound the name could have developed from the breton words lydd or ligg which means in or near water historically there have also been a number of alternative forms of the name including lihoumel which was attested as early as the twelfth century and lehowe which was mentioned in the sixteenth century lihou
