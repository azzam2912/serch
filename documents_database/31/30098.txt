originally the word referred to a literal bread board a polished piece of wood used for slicing bread in the 1970s the solderless breadboard plugboard a terminal array board became available and nowadays the term breadboard is commonly used to refer to these because the solderless breadboard does not require soldering it is reusable this makes it easy to use for creating temporary prototypes and experimenting with circuit design for this reason solderless breadboards are also popular with students and in technological education older breadboard types did not have this property a stripboard veroboard and similar prototyping printed circuit boards which are used to build semi permanent soldered prototypes or one offs cannot easily be reused a variety of electronic systems may be prototyped by using breadboards from small analog and digital circuits to complete central processing units cpus in the early days of radio amateurs nailed bare copper wires or terminal strips to a wooden board often literally a board to slice bread on and soldered electronic components to them sometimes a paper schematic diagram was first glued to the board as a guide to placing terminals then components and wires were installed over their symbols on the schematic
